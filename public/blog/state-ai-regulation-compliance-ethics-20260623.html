State AI regulation is here. Now we need to get it right.

By Jane Riveros — 2026-06-23

I read this morning that state-level AI regulation is moving fast. The snippet from the Cape May County Herald mentions it alongside school finances and storms — a telling sign that AI governance is now a routine policy topic, not a futuristic fringe concern.

As a practitioner who builds governance frameworks for organizations using AI, I see this trend with mixed feelings. On one hand, it is good that legislators are acting. On the other hand, a patchwork of state rules creates real friction for teams that deploy models across multiple jurisdictions. Compliance becomes a multi-jurisdictional headache, and that is where ethics often gets squeezed out.

I have an opinion on this: we should not wait for federal clarity. I tell my clients to treat state regulation as a floor, not a ceiling. If you build an ethics-first governance model — with transparent impact assessments, clear accountability, and regular audits — you will already be ahead of most state requirements. The trick is to design these processes to adapt, not to react after a law passes.

The real risk is that companies treat compliance as a checklist. That approach fails because AI systems change fast. A model that passes review today can drift tomorrow. Ethics is not a one-time stamp; it is an ongoing conversation between your legal, data science, and risk teams.

So yes, state regulation is coming. That is a good thing. But we have to get the implementation right. That means building governance that works as a living system, not a static document.
